## 1. The Setup

Berkshire Hathaway is composed of three financial pillars at YE2025:

1. **Wholly-owned operating businesses** generating $44.5B of after-tax operating earnings — diversified across insurance (GEICO, BHRG, BHPG), railroads (BNSF), utilities (BHE), aerospace (Precision Castparts), chemicals (Lubrizol), industrial (Marmon), retail (See's, Brooks, Pampered Chef, Dairy Queen), travel (Pilot Travel Centers, fully consolidated 2024), and distribution (McLane).
2. **Equity portfolio** of $274B at fair value (Q4 2025 13F): Apple 22.6%, American Express 20.5%, Bank of America 10.4%, Coca-Cola 10.2%, Chevron 7.2% — top 5 = ~71% of portfolio.
3. **Cash + U.S. Treasury bills** of $373.3B (with $321B in T-bills alone — Berkshire owns ~5% of the entire U.S. T-bill market). Insurance float of $176B (a record, +$5B YoY) sits inside this stack.

The defining 2026 transition: Warren Buffett stepped down as CEO on Dec 31, 2025; Greg Abel — VC, Non-Insurance since 2018 and CEO of BHE 2008–2018 — assumed the CEO role on Jan 1, 2026. Buffett remains Chairman; Ajit Jain continues to lead insurance.

The market's response: BRK.B is at –13.4% from its 52-week high while operating earnings doubled 2021–2025 ($27.6B → $44.5B). This is the cleanest "succession discount" mispricing setup available in mega-cap.

## 2. The Pivotal Question

> **Will Greg Abel deploy the $373B cash hoard at returns equal to or better than Buffett would have — and does the market's "succession discount" create a rare cost-basis opportunity in a fortress, or is the "Buffett premium" gone for good?**

Three observable proofs over the next 12–24 months will answer:
1. **Buyback cadence** — March 4, 2026 buyback at ~$469 was Abel's first signal that he views this zone as below intrinsic value. Pattern (size, frequency, valuation discipline) is the cleanest proxy for allocation skill.
2. **Acquisition pace** — does Abel deploy $30B–$70B into a major acquisition in 24 months, or does cash continue to grow toward $400B+?
3. **Insurance + GEICO turnaround** — float grew $5B in 2025 but GEICO's competitive position vs. Progressive tightened. Stabilizing GEICO is the nearest operational test.

## 4. Moat — Wide

Berkshire's moat compounds from five sources, none individually duplicable at scale:
- **Cost-of-float advantage**: $176B of insurance float at *negative* cost (paid to hold the money) is structurally cheaper than any debt or equity.
- **Capital allocation reputation + deal flow**: 60-year track record gives Berkshire access to deals others don't see (OXY 2020, GE 2008, Goldman 2008, Pilot 2017–2024).
- **Operating-business diversification + scale**: Each of BNSF, BHE, GEICO, PCC, Pilot, See's has its own moat; together they're irreplaceable.
- **Permanent capital, no redemption pressure**: Can wait 5+ years for the right deal.
- **Anti-fragility**: Designed to *benefit* from crises — only liquid white-knight buyer at scale.

**Vulnerabilities**:
- Succession risk (the dominant 2026 concern)
- GEICO competitive lag vs. Progressive
- Cash drag (every year of non-deployment is opportunity cost)
- Size (returns must moderate at $1T+)
- Insurance cycle starting to soften (Q4 2025 –54% underwriting profit)
