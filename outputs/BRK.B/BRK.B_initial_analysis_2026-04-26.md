## Moat: Wide and Structurally Peerless

Berkshire combines (a) $176B of negative-cost insurance float, (b) $373B of liquid dry powder, (c) ~$44B/yr of operating earnings from wholly-owned businesses, and (d) a 60-year permanent-capital culture. No public-market peer combines all four at $1T-cap. Closest analogues (Markel ~$25B, Fairfax ~$45B, Loews ~$20B) are useful only for relative valuation, not for moat comparison. Competitive risks are *internal* (succession, cash drag, GEICO operations), not external.

Primary-source moat citations:
- **Float economics**: *"continues to come at no cost to the company thanks to these sterling underwriting results"* — [Buffett FY2023 letter](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf).
- **Operating-business permanence**: *"A century from now, BNSF will continue to be a major asset of the country and of Berkshire."* — [Buffett FY2023 letter](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf).

## Primary-Source Synthesis (5-Year Letter Arc)

The FY2020 → FY2025 letters (Buffett's last 5 + Abel's first) reveal a consistent capital-allocation framework with one genuinely new operational lever under Abel.

**Key finding**: Cash growth from $138B (YE2020) → $373B (YE2025) was a *5-year demonstration of buyback discipline* (Buffett's *"stupid if done at a premium"* framework from FY2022), not capital-allocation paralysis. Abel's March 2026 buyback at ~$465–490 is therefore the inflection — *Abel's* assessment that intrinsic value is above this band, applying the same framework.

**New under Abel**: *"each percentage point improvement in operating margin generates approximately $230 million of incremental operating cash flow"* — [Abel FY2025 letter](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2025ltr.pdf). First time Berkshire has expressed an explicit operational lever in such concrete terms. **BNSF's 2025 +250bps margin expansion alone implies ~$575M of incremental cash flow** on Abel's framework — a tangible Year-1 win.
