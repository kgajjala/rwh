## Business Overview

DoorDash (NASDAQ: DASH) is the largest U.S. food-delivery and local-commerce marketplace, operating a three-sided platform connecting consumers, merchants (restaurants, grocers, retailers), and delivery drivers ("Dashers"). Founded by Tony Xu (Stanford, 2013) on the insight that local-commerce logistics was fragmented, expensive, and slow — and that a three-sided marketplace with an AI logistics engine could commoditize delivery, expanding the addressable market from ~$30B (food delivery) to >$1T (local convenience: groceries, retail, alcohol, pharmacy, restaurant tech).

**Scale (FY2025)**:
- **$13.72B revenue** (+28% YoY)
- **~$100B GOV** (pro-forma incl. Deliveroo partial year); Q4 2025: $29.7B (+39%)
- **2.58B+ orders FY**; Q4 2025: 903M (+32%)
- First sustained year of GAAP profitability: **$935M net income** (vs. $123M FY2024 = 7.6× growth)
- **$2.78B Adj EBITDA** (+52% YoY; ~20.3% margin); ~$1.8B FCF
- **56M MAUs** (Dec 2025); **35M subscribers** across DashPass + Wolt+ + Deliveroo Plus (+59% YoY)
- ~30K employees globally

**Geographic footprint** (post-Deliveroo close Oct 2, 2025):
- US: original market; 60–67% share of U.S. food-delivery
- International: ~40+ countries via Wolt (2022 acquisition) + Deliveroo (2025; UK + Belgium + France + Italy + Ireland + Kuwait + Qatar + Singapore + UAE)
- ~1B people addressable

**Revenue model** (5 streams):
- Marketplace commissions (15–30% of order value) — largest stream
- Consumer fees (delivery, service, small-order)
- Advertising (merchant promoted listings) — **fastest-growing, highest-margin layer**; Amazon-flywheel analog
- White-label logistics (DoorDash Drive / Wolt Drive)
- SaaS (SevenRooms restaurant CRM, acquired 2025 for ~$1.2B)

**2025 transformation**: Three M&A actions reframed DoorDash from "U.S. food-delivery #1" to "global local-commerce platform":
- **Deliveroo $3.9B all-cash** (closed Oct 2, 2025) — UK-anchored international expansion
- **SevenRooms $1.2B** — extends merchant wedge from delivery into restaurant CRM/reservations
- Q4 2025 reported as DASH's first quarter under combined entity

## Section 1 — Why Does This Company Exist? + Pivotal Investment Question

**Why it exists**: DoorDash was founded by Tony Xu in 2013 on the insight that local-commerce logistics — *moving any merchant's product to any consumer in under an hour* — was fragmented, expensive, and slow. The original mission, as articulated in Xu's [IPO letter (Dec 2020)](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1792789/000119312520292381/d752207ds1.htm), was *"to empower those like my Mom who came here with a dream to make it on their own"* and to *"build products that transform the way local merchants do business and enrich the communities in which they operate."* Food delivery was always the wedge, never the end state.

**Pivotal Investment Question**:

> Is DoorDash compounding into a durable, high-margin local-commerce platform — or is it the dominant player in a structurally low-margin, commodity-like category that requires perpetual capital infusion to defend?

The answer divides the scenario set:
- **Bull**: 15% Adj EBITDA margins on $22–25B of revenue by 2030 via advertising scale-up, international profitability through Deliveroo, and autonomous delivery flattening Dasher costs.
- **Bear**: U.S. dominance is permanent but international never reaches acceptable margins, ad monetization plateaus well below the Amazon analog, and gig-worker reclassification compresses unit economics.

## Section 5 — Competitive Moat

**Rating: Narrow** — three-sided network effects + scale dominance + subscription stickiness, but not switching-costs-and-IP. Requires continuous reinvestment to defend.

### Moat sources

| Source | Strength | Evidence |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Three-sided network effect | Strong | 56M MAU + 590K merchants + 9M+ Dashers in 2025 — largest U.S. consumer/merchant/Dasher base by wide margin |
| U.S. market share dominance | Strong | 60–67% (3× Uber Eats, 6× Grubhub); stable and marginally widening since 2020 despite Uber One bundling |
| DashPass / Wolt+ / Deliveroo Plus subscription | Moderate–Strong | **35M subscribers** (+59% YoY); ~30% of US MAUs ordering outside restaurants; higher order frequency + lower churn |
| AI logistics ("Deep Red") | Moderate | Proprietary ETA, routing, Dasher-matching; multi-year operational advantage hard to replicate without years of data |
| Advertising flywheel | Moderate (growing) | Highest-margin layer; Amazon-flywheel analog; underappreciated runway |

### Vulnerabilities

- **Consumer multi-homing is the norm** — true lock-in is limited; subscription stickiness is the only meaningful switching cost
- **Uber One bundling threat** is real in urban cores (NYC/LA/Chicago); Uber One has 46M subs (rides + delivery combined)
- **Gig-worker regulatory tail**: AB5-style federal reclassification would destroy current unit economics. Mitigated by [CA Supreme Court Prop 22 ruling July 2024](https://www.capradio.org/articles/2024/07/25/doordash-uber-and-other-gig-work-companies-can-classify-workers-as-contractors-ca-supreme-court-rules/), but not eliminated
- **Commission cap regulation**: NYC 15% / SF 20% commission caps are precedents; national cap would compress take rate

### How DoorDash's moat differs from competitors

1. **Scale-driven Dasher density** — DoorDash's 9M+ Dashers create the lowest delivery times + highest reliability, which compounds with consumer choice + merchant willingness to pay full-rate commission. Uber Eats can match individual cities but not the cross-category scale. **Evidence**: U.S. share has *widened* from ~60% to 67% during 2022–2025 *despite* aggressive Uber One bundling.
2. **Consolidated subscription scale** — 35M combined DashPass / Wolt+ / Deliveroo Plus subscribers gives DASH the largest delivery-subscription base globally outside Amazon Prime. Drives 30% of US MAUs to order outside restaurants — the foundation of the New Verticals (grocery/retail/alcohol) thesis. **Evidence**: subscriber growth +59% YoY in 2025; Q4 record.
3. **Wolt-precedent integration playbook** — DoorDash has executed one major international integration successfully (Wolt 2022 → profitable contributor by FY2025). This materially de-risks Deliveroo vs. peers like Uber Eats (Postmates integration was rocky). **Evidence**: Tony Xu's Q4 2025 framing that Deliveroo is *"growing much faster at the same profit contribution we expected"* at the 5-month mark.
4. **Vertical depth (food + grocery + retail + restaurant CRM simultaneously)** — DoorDash is the only major delivery platform building horizontally into restaurant CRM (SevenRooms) + grocery/retail + alcohol. Uber Eats is rides-led; Grubhub is restaurant-only; Instacart is grocery-only. **Evidence**: H2 2026 grocery/retail unit-economics inflection (per CFO Inukonda) is the dominant 2026 catalyst.

### Tail-risk competitor read

**Instacart (CART)** is the under-priced competitive risk. It dominates U.S. grocery delivery (~50%+) and is well-capitalized post-IPO. If Instacart acquires or partners with a restaurant-delivery player to build a 2-sided competitive set, DASH's grocery margin recovery thesis (H2 2026) is delayed. Not currently in consensus models.

## Section 9 — Industry-Specific Macro Analysis

**U.S. food delivery TAM**: ~$120–150B; DASH at 60–67% share on $100B+ GOV is approaching saturation in core category. Growth must come from (a) local-convenience expansion (grocery/retail/pharmacy/alcohol → $1T+ TAM) and (b) international.

**Structural dynamics**: The sector has consolidated to a DASH/Uber Eats duopoly in the U.S. (Grubhub is a distant third). Unit economics have improved dramatically — DASH went from –$3 per order to slightly positive as scale accumulated. Dasher cost per order is the core variable expense; autonomous delivery is the long-dated solution.

**Regulatory environment**: The two live risks are (1) city/state commission caps (NYC 15%, SF 20% — extending elsewhere?) and (2) federal gig-worker reclassification. A W-2 conversion of Dashers would add ~$3–5/delivery in labor costs — not survivable at current take rates without large consumer price increases, which would shrink volume.

**Consumer macro**: Food delivery is "nice to have" — order frequency is elastic to real disposable income. COVID proved delivery can spike; a deep recession would test base frequency.
