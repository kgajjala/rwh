## Business Overview

Uber Technologies (NYSE: UBER), founded 2009, operates a three-segment global on-demand platform: **Mobility** (ride-hailing in 70+ countries), **Delivery** (food, grocery, retail, alcohol — Eats brand at the core), and **Freight** (digital broker matching shippers/carriers).

**Scale (FY2025)**:
- **$52.0B revenue** (+18%); **$193.5B Gross Bookings** (+19%); **$8.7B Adj EBITDA** (+35%); **$9.8B FCF** (+42%)
- 199M monthly active platform consumers (Q1 2026); 13.6B trips (FY2025, +20%)
- 70+ countries; ~10,000+ cities; millions of drivers and couriers
- $27.0B total buyback authorization ($20.5B remaining post-Q1)

**Revenue mix (FY2025)**: Mobility 57% / Delivery 33% / Freight 10%. Q1 2026 inflection: Delivery GB ($26.0B) **nearly matched Mobility GB ($26.4B) for first time** — structural shift toward delivery parity.

**Geographic mix**: US/Canada ~60%, EMEA ~21%, LatAm ~10%, APAC ~9%. International revenue diversifies macro exposure but adds regulatory complexity (EU Platform Work Directive, UK status rulings).

**Strategic positioning**: Post-2024 the playbook decisively shifted toward **superapp + AV distribution layer**. Uber One subscription crossed 50M members (+50% YoY), driving 50%+ of bookings with members spending 3× non-members — Costco-like flywheel. Simultaneously, Uber signed 30+ AV partnerships (Waymo, Zoox, Hertz/Nuro/Lucid, Aurora) — positioning as the demand-and-distribution platform that AV operators need to monetize hardware, not as an AV developer (Uber sold ATG to Aurora 2020). The 2026 product expansions (Expedia hotels, Blacklane chauffeurs) signal a deliberate shift from ride-hailing to vertically-integrated travel platform.

## Competitive Moat & Landscape

**Rating**: **Wide (Network Effects + Brand)**

The two-sided network — driver supply density determines wait time, wait time determines retention, demand reinforces driver supply — is geographically local and structurally hard to replicate. Uber's 70-country footprint compounds: each new market it enters from platform muscle (capital, brand, operations) is harder to dislodge than a standing-start local entrant.

**Reinforcing moat sources**:
- Uber One subscription (50M members, 3× spend) — demand-side switching cost that didn't exist in 2022
- Data/algorithm advantage trained on 13.6B trips and ~$1T cumulative GB
- Capital scale: $9.8B FCF funds AV partnerships, market entry, regulatory absorption
- Driver multi-homing economics tilt to Uber on utilization

**Live debate — AV row**:

| Direction | Live Position |
|---|---|
| **Bull** | AV operators need ~$2B+ cumulative consumer-acquisition spend to build a two-sided demand network from scratch; they choose to share take-rate with Uber. 30+ partnerships including Waymo (the AV leader) ratify this. |
| **Bear** | AV economics (no driver, no surge labor) generate enough margin to justify the build; once built, operators bypass Uber. Waymo's standalone app already exists. |

The Q1 2026 commentary on "Uber Autonomous Solutions" — fleet management, financing, insurance — is explicitly positioning Uber as the **operating layer AV operators need**. Whether this is sufficient depends on AV unit economics that remain unknown.
