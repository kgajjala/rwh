## Business Overview

UnitedHealth Group (NYSE: UNH) is the largest U.S. health insurance and healthcare services company by revenue ($447.6B in FY2025). It operates two primary segments: **UnitedHealthcare** (~$344.9B FY2025 revenue) — the insurance/managed care arm covering employer-sponsored commercial, individual, Medicare Advantage (MA), Medicaid, and global plans, with 49.8M medical members at year-end 2025; and **Optum** (~$270.6B FY2025 revenue, partially intersegment), which spans Optum Health (value-based care clinics, employed physicians, surgical centers), Optum Insight (data analytics, consulting, Change Healthcare), and Optum Rx (the #2 PBM by script volume).

The vertical integration thesis — insurer + PBM + care delivery + analytics — is what differentiates UNH from pure insurers. In 2025 this thesis faced its hardest test on record: Medical Loss Ratio (MLR) hit 89.1% (vs. ~85.5% historical), CEO Andrew Witty resigned in May 2025 with prior CEO Stephen Hemsley returning, FY2025 guidance was suspended, Optum Health adjusted earnings collapsed from $7.9B to $2.3B, and the company faces an ongoing DOJ antitrust investigation into the integrated structure. The stock fell from a 52-week high of $424.12 (recently re-tested) to a recent low of $234.60 — and now sits at $354.92, reflecting a partial recovery from the lows but persistent volatility.

## Moat Assessment

**Wide** — durable, multi-source, but currently under near-term pressure on its expression.

**Sources**:
- **Scale**: #1 insurer by revenue and membership; cost advantages in underwriting and provider negotiation
- **Switching costs**: Employer-sponsored plan switches occur on 2–3 year contract cycles with high administrative cost; individual members rarely mid-year switch
- **Data advantages**: 50M+ members × decades of claims, Rx, lab, and clinical data = the most comprehensive actuarial dataset in U.S. healthcare
- **Optum care network**: 90,000+ employed/affiliated physicians, surgical centers, infusion clinics — creates stickiness for UHC members AND for competing payers that contract with Optum
- **Regulatory moat**: 50-state insurance licensure, ERISA compliance, PBM relationships — multi-year barriers to entry

**Vulnerabilities**:
- **DOJ antitrust investigation** into vertical integration — the tail risk is forced Optum divestiture (low probability, very high impact)
- **CMS Medicare Advantage rate-setting** — annual regulatory squeeze; FY2025 rates were unfavorable; FY2026 modestly improved
- **Political / reputational exposure**: Brian Thompson assassination (Dec 2024) created historic reputational scrutiny — without precedent in managed care
- **Change Healthcare cyberattack (Feb 2024)**: continuing claims-processing disruption fallout; civil liability and regulatory consequences still being assessed
- **MLR durability question**: Whether 2025's 89.1% reflects cyclical Medicare Advantage cost surge (recoverable) or structural post-COVID population health worsening (permanent margin reset)

## Section 1 — Why Does This Company Exist? + Pivotal Investment Question

**Founding insight**: U.S. healthcare is catastrophically fragmented. Employers, patients, physicians, hospitals, and pharmacies operate in disconnected silos, driving costs up and quality down. UNH's insight (built over decades) is that vertical integration — insurer + PBM + care delivery + analytics — creates a closed-loop system that can reduce costs, improve outcomes, and capture value at every step of the healthcare dollar.

**Today**: UNH is a $447.6B revenue, 49.8M-member integrated healthcare ecosystem. The "integrated UNH" — Optum + UnitedHealthcare working in concert — was supposed to transform UNH from an insurance company into a diversified healthcare conglomerate. In 2025 that thesis was tested as never before.

**Pivotal question**: Is the 2025 margin collapse cyclical (recovering 2026–2027 with Hemsley's discipline + member repricing) or structural (DOJ unraveling + permanent MLR reset)? The market currently prices a partial recovery; the asymmetry depends on which scenario actually materializes.

**Business model mechanics**:
- UNH collects premiums from employers, government (CMS), and individuals

- Spread: 100% premium – 89.1% claims – ~6% opex ≈ ~5% operating margin in 2025 (vs. ~8% normalized)
- Optum layers on care delivery + PBM + analytics — capturing value across the continuum in theory; in 2025 Optum Health was a drag rather than a contributor

**Sensitivity**: 100bps MLR improvement = ~$4–5B EBIT improvement at UNH's scale.

## Section 9 — Industry-Specific Macro Analysis

**Medical Cost Ratio / utilization** is the dominant variable. 2025's 89.1% MLR vs. historical 82–85% represents ~400bps of excess losses. Drivers: post-COVID behavioral health surge, MA demographic age-up, prior-auth changes mandated by CMS, Medicaid redetermination driving higher-acuity enrollment. Sensitivity: 100bps MLR improvement = ~$4–5B EBIT.

**Medicare Advantage CMS rate-setting** is the critical annual variable. 2025 MA rates were approximately flat/unfavorable; 2026 MA rates announced March 2025 were modestly improved. Each 1% favorable rate change ≈ $1–2B EBIT impact for the MA book.

**Medicaid policy**: Post-COVID unwinding of expanded Medicaid eligibility has driven higher-acuity enrollment remaining in Medicaid programs — increasing costs. State-level rebidding creates periodic revenue/margin risk.

**Interest-rate environment** (secondary): UNH holds significant investment portfolio; higher rates = higher investment income, partially offsetting MLR pressure.
