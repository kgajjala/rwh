1. Five-Year Horizon (March 2021 → March 2026)

Total Price Return: From ~$123.50 (Mar 2021) to $143.35 (Mar 2026) = +16.1%
Annualized price CAGR: ~3.0% | Including dividends (~3% yield): ~+35% total return (~6.3% annualized). S&P 500 5-yr annualized: ~13% (significantly underperforms).

5-Year Context: Five years ago, PG was a $290B company delivering steady organic sales growth of 5-7%, benefiting from pricing power and brand loyalty. Today it's a $334B company struggling to grow sales at 0-4% organically, navigating tariff headwinds, a CEO transition, a major restructuring, and consumer trade-down pressure. The fundamentals haven't broken — it's still a Dividend King with $84B in revenue — but the stock's multiple has compressed from ~26x to ~20x as growth decelerated. This is a story of mean reversion in a richly-valued defensve stock.

Phase 1: The Pandemic-Era Premium (March 2021 – January 2022)

PG rose from ~$123.50 to ~$161 (+30%) as investors crowded into consumer staples during the economic uncertainty of COVID's tail-end. Revenue surged on pricing power and household spending tailwinds. The stock reached a then-record of ~$161 in January 2022, trading at a premium ~26x P/E. EXT-MARKET

Phase 2: The 2022 Bear Market Drawdown (January – October 2022)

PG fell ~24% alongside the broader market as the Fed began the most aggressive rate-hiking cycle in 40 years. But unlike pure growth stocks, PG's decline was amplified by a company-specific issue: commodity cost inflation (crude oil, resins, pulp, freight) hit PG's margins hard. Operating income fell even as revenue rose. The July 2022 earnings were a particular wake-up call — EPS missed estimates for the first time in years. Management warned of "significant headwinds" in FY2023. EXT-MACRO + INT-FUND

Phase 3: The Recovery and Re-Rating (October 2022 – April 2025)

The stock recovered from its ~$122 October 2022 trough to an all-time high of ~$174-180 by November 2024–April 2025 (+43%). This 30-month rally was driven by: (1) inflation peaking and rate cut cycle beginning; (2) PG's organic sales recovering to 3-7% range through 2023-2024; (3) defensive rotation as recession fears lingered; (4) multiple expansion as rates fell. The stock hit a 52-week high of $174.80 on April 3, 2025 and an all-time closing high near $173-180 in late 2024. EXT-MACRO + INT-FUND

Phase 4: The Fundamental Deterioration Drawdown (April 2025 – January 2026)

From the April 2025 high (~$174) to the January 2026 low ($137.62), PG fell 21% — right on the 20%+ threshold. Unlike Phase 2 (which was largely extrinsic), this decline was driven by genuine intrinsic deterioration: multiple earnings disappoints, a guidance cut citing tariffs, a CEO change, a restructuring announcement, and consumer trade-down to private labels. The stock hit a 52-week and multi-year low of $137.62 on January 7, 2026. INT-FUND

Phase 5: Volatile Recovery and Re-Selloff (January 2026 – Present)

After the January 2026 low, PG rallied nearly 19% to $163.14 by February 23, 2026 — driven by defensive rotation as tech/AI sold off. But the stock has since given back more than half of those gains, falling to $143.35 today on a combination of macro uncertainty (Iran war fears, "sell America" trade rethinking) and continued fundamental questions about growth. Event Analysis — Deep Dives on Key Intrinsic Moves

April 24, 2025: Q3 FY2025 Guidance Cut — Tariffs & Consumer Uncertainty
INT-FUND (Primary)  EXT-MACRO (Secondary)
-4.6% single day, then -14% over subsequent months
What Happened

P&G reported Q3 FY2025 results that technically beat EPS, but the company simultaneously lowered its full-year FY2025 guidance, citing "consumer uncertainty" and tariff headwinds of $1–1.5B per year. CEO Moeller told CNBC: "Tariffs are inherently inflationary." P&G indicated mid-single-digit price increases on about a quarter of its products would be necessary in FY2026. This was the pivot point where the market recognized PG was not immune to the tariff environment.

Market's Interpretation

The market read this as a signal that PG's pricing power — the very foundation of its Wide Moat — was now a double-edged sword. Raising prices to offset tariffs risked accelerating volume losses to private-label alternatives, which were already gaining ground. The stock was trading near its all-time high (~$173) when the news hit; a guidance cut at a peak multiple is always punished severely.

Assessment — Was the Market Right?

Largely yes, though the cumulative 21% decline from April 2025 to January 2026 may have overshot the fundamental reality. The tariff impact of $1–1.5B on a company with $85B revenue and ~$15.7B net income is meaningful but not existential (~8-10% of net income). The deeper concern the market correctly identified: organic volume growth was already slowing before tariffs. Tariffs just made an already-difficult story worse. The restructuring announcement (7,000 job cuts) in June 2025 — framed as proactive — was interpreted as a confession of structural weakness.

Lasting Impact

Permanent compression in P/E from ~25–26x to ~20–21x, reflecting a structurally lower organic growth outlook. The tariff headwind also forced a CEO transition and restructuring that will generate near-term charges and execution uncertainty through 2026–2027.

July 28–29, 2025: CEO Jon Moeller Replaced by Shailesh Jejurikar
INT-PEOPLE (Primary)  INT-FUND (Secondary)
-4.3% on announcement day
What Happened

On July 28, 2025, P&G announced Moeller would be replaced by COO Shailesh Jejurikar, effective January 1, 2026. Moeller became Executive Chairman. The announcement came one day before Q4 FY2025 results. Management framed it as a "planned, orderly transition." The simultaneous Q4 results showed revenue essentially flat at $84.3B and EPS guidance for FY2026 that included the $1B tariff headwind. The restructuring plan (7,000 jobs, brand divestitures, market exits) was already underway.

Market's Interpretation

Despite the "orderly transition" framing, the market was uneasy. The timing — mid-restructuring, amid ongoing performance challenges — raised questions about whether the board had lost confidence in Moeller's execution. Jejurikar, a 36-year company veteran and COO, was seen as a continuity pick rather than a change agent, which muted enthusiasm. Brian Jacobsen (Annex Wealth Management) captured the market's ambivalence: "This could be more like the passing of the baton in a long race rather than shaking things up."

Assessment — Was the Market Right?

The market's cautious reaction was proportionate. Jejurikar is a competent operator, but a change at the top without a clear new strategic direction provides limited re-rating catalyst. The "passing of the baton" is the right analogy — no new leadership premium was warranted. The real question (as yet unanswered) is whether Jejurikar will execute more aggressively on portfolio pruning or accelerate AI-driven efficiency gains. His first earnings (Q2 FY2026, Jan 22, 2026) showed EPS of $1.78 vs. $1.87 expected — not a confidence-building debut.

Lasting Impact

Near-term uncertainty elevated through at least H1 FY2026. Market will need 2–3 earnings cycles under Jejurikar before re-rating the stock on management quality. The restructuring charges ($1.5–2.0B over FY2026–2027) will create GAAP noise for the next 12–18 months.

October 2022: 2022 Bear Market Trough — Inflation Peak / Rate Hike Cycle
EXT-MACRO (Primary)  INT-FUND (Secondary)
-24.2% peak-to-trough (Jan → Oct 2022)
What Happened

PG fell from ~$161 (Jan 2022) to ~$122 (Oct 2022), roughly tracking the broader market's -22% decline over the same period. The proximate cause was the Fed's 425bps rate hike cycle and the repricing of all equities at higher discount rates. For PG specifically, the secondary driver was genuine margin compression: commodity costs (oil, resins, pulp) surged 15-20%, freight costs doubled, and foreign exchange was a $3.7B headwind in FY2023. Management warned explicitly of "another year of significant headwinds" in July 2022.

Market's Interpretation

The market correctly identified that even defensive staples companies aren't immune to rate repricing. A company trading at 26x earnings needs rates at 0-1% to justify that premium; at 4%+, the earnings yield (1/26 = 3.8%) no longer looks attractive. The combination of multiple compression AND fundamental pressure created the full -24% move.

Assessment — Was the Market Right?

Yes, and the subsequent recovery proved the thesis. Once inflation peaked and the Fed signaled rate cuts were coming, PG recovered all losses and then some within 2 years. The fundamental "damage" (margin compression) was temporary — PG's organic sales growth recovered to 4-7% in FY2023-2024 as commodity costs normalized. Hindsight confirms October 2022 was an excellent long-term entry point.

Lasting Impact

Temporary disruption. PG recovered to all-time highs by 2024–2025. The 2022 episode demonstrated that even a 26x P/E defensive stock can lose 24% if the rate environment shifts materially. This is the key valuation lesson for PG: the premium multiple is contingent on a relatively low-rate environment.

🔍 Event Pattern Takeaway

This stock's major moves are driven by both macro and intrinsic factors. ⚖️ Entry Point Assessment

At ~$143 and ~20x forward earnings (FY2026E EPS ~$7.10–7.20):

  • The dividend yield (~2.94%) is at its highest since 2019 — income investors find PG increasingly attractive at this level.
  • Historical support zones: The $137–140 range (52-wk low territory) is the key technical floor. A break below $137 with conviction would suggest further downside to the $125–130 range (2022-era levels).

Bottom Line: PG is in a "show me" mode under new CEO Jejurikar. The stock is neither screaming cheap nor obviously overvalued. For a value-oriented investor focused on dividend income and capital preservation, $137–143 offers a meaningful entry with the dividend yield as a floor. For a growth-oriented investor, the more compelling entry would be post-Q3 FY2026 confirmation that organic growth is re-accelerating.
