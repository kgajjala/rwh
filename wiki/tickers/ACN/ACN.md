## Business Overview

Accenture is the world's largest professional-services firm by revenue (FY25 revenue $69.7B; ~786,000 employees as of Q2 FY26), providing strategy, consulting, technology, operations, Song (marketing/experience) and Industry X (engineering) services to the world's largest enterprises and governments. It serves more than 9,000 clients including ~75% of the Fortune Global 500, and has partnered with 195 of its top 200 clients for 10+ years. The business is structurally capital-light: capex is < $0.7B/year against ~$11B+ operating cash flow, so value compounds through revenue growth, margin, buybacks and dividends rather than reinvested physical capital.

Effective September 1, 2025, Accenture consolidated its delivery capabilities into a single integrated business unit, **Reinvention Services**, and reports through three geographic markets (Americas, EMEA, Asia Pacific) and five industry groups (Communications/Media/Tech, Financial Services, Health & Public Service, Products, Resources). Its current strategic vision is to be its clients' "reinvention partner of choice" in the age of AI — embedding data and generative AI across all engagements while continuing to rotate its skill base with each technology wave (cloud → digital → GenAI), funded by ~$3B of multi-year GenAI investment and ~$5B of FY26 acquisitions.

## Pivotal Investment Question

> **Does generative AI structurally shrink the consulting/IT-services revenue pool by collapsing the labor-arbitrage and staff-augmentation model — or does Accenture's scale, ecosystem partnerships and reinvention positioning let it monetize AI as a *net new* spend category faster than it cannibalizes legacy work?**

The market is pricing the first outcome (multiple at a multi-year trough). The bookings, share-gain and AI-revenue data so far support the second — but the deceleration in headline growth is real and the debate is genuinely open.

## Section 3 — Competitive Moat & Landscape

**Moat: Wide.**

Moat sources, with evidence:
- **Switching costs / embedded relationships**: 195 of top 200 clients retained 10+ years; multi-year managed-services contracts and deep systems integration make rip-and-replace costly ([FY25 letter](../../../raw/ACN/shareholder-letters/2025_letter.pdf)).
- **Scale / breadth**: ~786,000 people across 52 countries; able to staff multi-service, multi-geography "reinvention at scale" programs few rivals can match; 74 $100M+ client bookings in H1 FY26.
- **Ecosystem intangibles**: top-tier partner status with all major hyperscalers/software vendors (AWS, Microsoft/Azure, Google, SAP, ServiceNow, NVIDIA) — partner-sourced revenue outgrows the firm.
- **Reinvestment flywheel**: ~$3B GenAI investment, ~$0.8B R&D, ~$1.0B learning/development annually, ~$5B/yr acquisitions — funded internally at high ROIC because the model is capital-light.

Where the moat faces pressure: generative AI compresses the value of *headcount-based* delivery (the historical labor-arbitrage engine); if clients can self-serve transformation with AI tooling, the consulting value-capture could structurally shrink — this is the thesis-defining vulnerability and the reason the multiple has de-rated. Chronic (not yet existential): pricing pressure in legacy/commoditized services.

How Accenture differs and the evidence: it is the only player combining *full breadth* (strategy → Song → Industry X → operations) at *global scale* with *premium positioning* and *internal reinvestment capacity*. Evidence the differentiator holds: it claims share gains at ~5x its closest publicly traded peers (its own market-share metric) and a durable ~1.2 book-to-bill even through the FY23–24 slowdown. Honest tail-risk read: if AI commoditizes the *integration* layer (not just the labor layer), scale-and-breadth converts from a moat into a cost base — Accenture's defense is that AI makes orchestration *more* complex, favoring the largest integrator, but this is an assertion the next 4–8 quarters must validate.

## Section 7 — Industry-Specific Macro Analysis

Dominant macro variables: (1) **enterprise IT / discretionary consulting budgets** and (2) **the pace of generative-AI enterprise adoption**.

- **IT/consulting budgets**: Pro-cyclical with corporate confidence and rates. The FY23–24 episode demonstrated sensitivity — a ~1–2 s.d. tightening in discretionary budgets cut Accenture's growth from mid-teens to ~+1% within ~18 months, with EBITDA growth compressing commensurately though margins held via cost discipline. Backlog (book-to-bill ~1.2) is the shock absorber.
- **GenAI adoption pace**: A *positive* macro variable for the bull, a *negative* for the bear. Faster enterprise GenAI adoption = larger reinvention/transformation TAM and more orchestration complexity favoring the largest integrator (GenAI bookings already $5.9B FY25). The risk is asymmetric in *form*: AI expands the addressable transformation spend but could compress the *value captured per unit of delivery* if it deflates the headcount-hours model.

TAM context: global IT-services + consulting is a multi-trillion-dollar spend pool; Accenture at ~$70B revenue holds low-single-digit share, so the structural question is value-capture per dollar of client transformation, not market saturation. Regulatory environment is benign for the core business (Irish domicile; government-client compliance is the main idiosyncratic exposure).
