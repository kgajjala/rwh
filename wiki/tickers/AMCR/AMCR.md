## Business Overview

Amcor plc is the global leader in consumer packaging and dispensing solutions, formed through the April 30, 2025 all-stock combination of legacy Amcor (founded 1860; incorporated in Jersey 2018 for NYSE listing) and Berry Global Group. The combined company employs ≈77,000 people, operates 400+ manufacturing facilities across 40+ countries, and serves ≈140 countries with ≈$22B in annualized revenue. Amcor is incorporated in Jersey (Channel Islands), domiciled for tax and management purposes partly in Switzerland and partly in the US; the company has been a domestic SEC 10-K filer since FY2019 (not a foreign-private-issuer 20-F filer), with primary listing on NYSE and secondary listing on ASX.

The company operates in two segments post-Berry merger: **Global Flexible Packaging Solutions** (legacy Amcor Flexibles + Berry's global flexible operations; food/beverage/healthcare/home & personal care flexible films, laminates, bags) and **Global Rigid Packaging Solutions** (legacy Amcor Rigid + Berry's Consumer Packaging International and Consumer Packaging North America; bottles, containers, closures for food, beverage, household, beauty). Amcor's business model is a **capital-intensive process manufacturer with long-term customer contracts**: it converts resins, films, foils, and other substrates into proprietary packaging structures at its own plants under multi-year supply agreements, typically with raw-material pass-through clauses that allow margin-on-conversion economics to be insulated from commodity cost swings. Switching costs are moderate — qualification of new suppliers for FDA-regulated or high-tolerance formats takes 12–24 months — which creates meaningful but not impenetrable customer retention.

### Business Model

Amcor is a **margin-on-conversion** manufacturer: it purchases resins/aluminum/paperboard/films as raw materials and charges customers for the value-added conversion (design, engineering, specialized coatings, shelf-life extension). Most long-term contracts embed raw-material pass-through mechanisms, meaning commodity cost swings affect revenue more than margins. Margin compression occurs when (a) volumes fall (fixed-cost absorption deteriorates), (b) pass-through lag is imperfect (2–3 quarter lag in some contracts), or (c) mix shifts to lower-value product lines. The post-Berry combined company improves purchasing scale on resins (top-5 global buyer of many polymers) and cross-sells proprietary formats across the combined customer base.

## 3. Competitive Moat & Landscape

### Moat Rating: **Narrow (Scale + Customer Switching Costs)**

Amcor does not possess a wide moat. It is not a natural monopoly, platform, or brand business. Its competitive advantages are:

1. **Manufacturing scale**: Post-Berry, Amcor is the largest or second-largest player in most global flexible-packaging categories. Scale advantages in resin purchasing (top-5 global buyer), logistics, and manufacturing overhead absorption are real but replicable at sufficient capital investment.
2. **Customer qualification switching costs**: In FDA-regulated (pharmaceutical, food contact) and high-tolerance flexible packaging, switching suppliers requires a 12–24 month validation and qualification process. This creates inertia but not lock-in — a sufficiently differentiated competitor can pry customers away with a lengthy sales cycle.
3. **Geographic footprint**: 400+ facilities in 40+ countries allows Amcor to serve global CPG customers (Unilever, P&G, Nestlé, Coca-Cola, J&J) across all their operating regions under a single global supply agreement — a significant operational convenience that smaller competitors cannot match.
4. **Proprietary packaging structures**: Patent and trade-secret protected formats (barrier films, reclosable pouches, specialty healthcare formats) create narrow category-specific moats. These are real but expire; competitors invest in R&D to replicate.

The moat is **not** wide because: (a) packaging is a mature industry with many capable global and regional players; (b) raw-material pass-through contracts limit pricing power; (c) customer concentration (top 10 CPG customers likely represent 30–40% of sales, unverified) creates negotiation leverage on the customer side; (d) end-market commoditization in standard film packaging means price is a primary purchasing criterion.

### CEO Transition Context

Ron Delia served as CEO for 9 years (≈2015–April 2024) before stepping down for health reasons. Delia's retirement coincided with the Berry merger announcement (November 2023) — the timing raises a question about succession planning during a transformative transaction, but the Berry deal was ultimately approved and closed under Konieczny's leadership. Peter Konieczny's appointment formalized in September 2024 after a six-month interim period — the board confirmed the inside candidate, avoiding a disruptive external search during active integration.

Stephen Scherger replacing previous CFO Michael Casamento (who departed with Delia's cohort) in November 2025 adds another leadership change during a critical integration window. Scherger's Graphic Packaging tenure (packaging-sector CFO through a major M&A cycle) is relevant experience.

### Regulatory Environment

- **EU PPWR** (effective Feb 2025): Sets design-for-recyclability requirements; PCR content minimums (35% non-sensitive plastic by 2030, 10% food-contact by 2030); PFAS-in-food restrictions from August 2026. Amcor 94% recyclable flexible design, 233,000 MT PCR used — ahead of many peers. Still requires ongoing investment. Source: [Amcor PPWR blog](https://www.amcor.com/insights/blogs/eu-regulatory-outlook-2026).
- **US plastic packaging regulations**: California, New York, and other states have enacted extended producer responsibility (EPR) schemes. Less comprehensive than EU but expanding. Amcor participates in industry coalitions.
- **Antitrust scrutiny**: The Berry merger received global antitrust clearances (US DOJ, EU, Australia, others) — no material structural remedies required. Future large-scale consolidation will face higher scrutiny given combined market position.
