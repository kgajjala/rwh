## Business Overview

Kodiak Gas Services (NYSE: KGS) is the largest contract natural gas compression services provider in the United States, with a fleet of approximately **4.35 million revenue-generating horsepower** at year-end 2025 (98% utilization — industry-leading). Kodiak owns and operates large-horsepower compression units that are deployed at customer well sites, gathering systems, and midstream facilities to move natural gas through pipelines, gather associated gas in oily basins (notably the Permian), and enable LNG and gas-fired power generation feedstock flow. The business model is fee-based, take-or-pay style contracts with predominantly investment-grade midstream and E&P customers — economics resemble a utility more than a cyclical oilfield services name.

In 2024 Kodiak completed the transformative acquisition of **CSI Compressco** (~580 thousand horsepower added), making it the clear #1 in U.S. compression. In April 2026, Kodiak announced the acquisition of **Distributed Power Solutions (DPS)** for ~$675M — a strategic pivot into behind-the-meter natural-gas-fired distributed power generation, primarily targeting AI data center load growth. FY2025 revenue was $1.30B (+13% YoY) with adjusted EBITDA of $715M (+17%); operating cash flow of $599.7M funded both the dividend and CapEx without straining leverage.

Revenue is overwhelmingly concentrated in the **Compression Operations** segment (~95% of revenue), with a small **Other Services** segment (station construction, maintenance). Compression is contracted on multi-year fixed-fee agreements with annual escalators; revenue is largely insulated from spot natural gas price moves.

## Moat Assessment

**Narrow** — scale + asset specificity + customer-switching friction in an oligopoly structure.

**Sources**:
- **Scale leadership**: 4.35M HP fleet — largest in U.S. contract compression after CSI Compressco merger; ~25–30% market share
- **Large-horsepower specialization**: average 970 HP per unit, skewed to >1,500 HP "super-large" units that midstream operators in the Permian require for high-pressure gathering and gas lift — a niche where Kodiak has the deepest fleet
- **Asset specificity**: large compressors are custom-spec'd, multi-year lead time to manufacture; 98% fleet utilization means new demand has to wait or pay premium
- **Customer concentration with investment-grade counterparties**: top customers are large, credit-worthy midstreams (e.g., Williams, Energy Transfer, Targa) — switching is operationally painful and rare once units are deployed
- **Take-or-pay contracts** with annual escalators insulate revenue from gas-price cyclicality — economics resemble a regulated utility

**Vulnerabilities**:
- **Cyclical end-market**: ultimate driver of compression demand is U.S. natural gas production growth; a sustained Permian slowdown (gas-price collapse, OPEC+ surprise) would compress utilization toward 85%
- **Capital intensity**: building horsepower is expensive; unleveraged ROIC depends on multi-year contract attachment
- **Leverage**: post-CSI Compressco and post-DPS, balance sheet is moderately levered (~3.5–4.0× net debt / EBITDA) — exposed to refinancing cost shocks
- **DPS execution risk**: distributed power is a new vertical; Kodiak is a compression operator, not a power-gen specialist, and the AI data center demand thesis is still being underwritten

## Section 1 — Why Does This Company Exist? + Pivotal Investment Question

**Founding insight**: U.S. shale gas production grew faster than midstream operators could build owned compression. Outsourcing large-horsepower compression to a specialized fleet operator was both faster and cheaper than self-build. Kodiak focused on **large-horsepower** units (>1,500 HP) suited to the Permian's high-pressure gathering and gas-lift workflows — a deliberate specialization that produced higher utilization and lower per-HP servicing cost than competitors operating mixed fleets.

**Today**: Kodiak is the #1 U.S. contract compression operator at 4.35M HP with a 98% utilization rate. The CSI Compressco merger (2024) gave it scale; the DPS acquisition (April 2026) extends the asset base into behind-the-meter natural-gas power generation aimed at AI data center load growth — a natural adjacency given Kodiak's expertise in deploying and operating large reciprocating engines in remote field locations.

**Pivotal question**: Is the natural-gas-compression / behind-the-meter-power thesis a 5–7 year structural up-cycle (Permian associated-gas + LNG + AI power) where Kodiak compounds FCF and de-levers, or are we mid-2026 buying near a cycle top?

## Section 9 — Industry-Specific Macro Analysis

**TAM**: U.S. installed contract compression HP ~17–18M; total addressable HP (including owned-and-operated by E&Ps and midstreams) ~75M. Kodiak's 4.35M = ~25% of contract market.

**Structural drivers**:
1. **Permian associated-gas growth**: Crude production up → associated gas up → compression demand up. Permian gas-to-oil ratio is rising as wells age, structurally bullish for compression.
2. **LNG export build-out**: U.S. LNG capacity expanding from ~14 Bcf/d to ~25 Bcf/d by 2028; pulls Haynesville and Permian volumes through gas-takeaway compression.
3. **AI data center power demand**: Behind-the-meter natural gas generation is the fastest-deployable power source; KGS-via-DPS positioned to capitalize.
4. **Pipeline-to-power**: Power generation increasingly natural-gas-fired (renewables intermittency); persistent compression demand.

**Cyclicality**: The 2014–2016 oil collapse pushed compression utilization to ~80%. Today's 98% leaves zero slack — the next cycle low could be more painful since there's no incremental demand to absorb.

**Regulatory environment**: Methane mitigation rules push customers toward modern, lower-emission compressors — a tailwind for Kodiak's relatively young fleet.
