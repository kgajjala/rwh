## Business Description

The Procter & Gamble Company (NYSE: PG) is one of the world's largest consumer goods
companies, a Dividend King with over 66 consecutive years of dividend increases. PG sells
branded consumer products across 10 categories including Fabric & Home Care (Tide, Downy,
Febreze), Baby, Feminine & Family Care (Pampers, Charmin, Bounty), Beauty (Head & Shoulders,
Pantene, Olay), Grooming (Gillette, Braun), Health Care (Oral-B, Vicks, Pepto-Bismol), and
Personal Health Care (Metamucil, Crest, Scope).

Revenues of ~$84B annually. Net income ~$15.7B. Employs approximately 107,000 people.
Operates in ~70 countries, sells in 180+ countries.

**Business model**: Brand-premium pricing. PG's competitive advantage is brand equity,
distribution scale, and retail shelf presence that allows it to charge 20–40% premiums over
private-label alternatives. Revenue is subscription-like in nature — consumers re-purchase
household necessities repeatedly, providing extreme stability but limiting high-growth
potential.

**CEO Transition**: Jon Moeller replaced by Shailesh Jejurikar (36-year company veteran,
former COO) effective January 1, 2026. Moeller became Executive Chairman. The transition
occurred mid-restructuring amid ongoing performance challenges.

## Competitive Moat

**Assessment**: Wide Moat (but under pressure)

**Sources of Moat**:

1. **Brand equity**: Household names with decades of consumer loyalty. Tide, Pampers, Gillette
   command premium pricing and top retail shelf position globally. Difficult to replicate without
   decades of advertising investment.

2. **Distribution scale**: Global distribution network reaching 180+ countries. Retail
   relationships and shelf-space dominance create barriers for new entrants.

3. **R&D and innovation**: Continuous product innovation (fabric softener technology, diaper
   absorption, razor blade engineering) maintains performance gaps over private-label.

4. **Switching costs (moderate)**: Consumer habit formation and brand loyalty create moderate
   stickiness, though lower than technology switching costs. Private-label pressure is real
   during consumer downturns.

**Moat Concern**: Private-label alternatives are gaining market share as consumers trade down
amid inflation-driven price increases. PG's pricing power — the foundation of the Wide Moat —
is now a double-edged sword: raising prices to offset tariff headwinds risks accelerating
volume losses. Organic volume growth was slowing before tariffs arrived.

## Pivotal Investment Question

> Is PG's 2025–2026 drawdown (from ~$174 to ~$143) a macro-driven overreaction to temporary
> tariff headwinds and a leadership transition — creating a value entry for a Dividend King with
> durable brand equity — or does it reflect genuine structural deterioration (private-label
> encroachment, permanent pricing power erosion) that justifies a permanently lower multiple?

The stock is in "show me" mode under new CEO Jejurikar. Q3 FY2026 earnings (April 24, 2026)
will be the first meaningful test of whether organic growth can re-accelerate to 3–5% and
whether restructuring savings are materializing.
