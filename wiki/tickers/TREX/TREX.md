## Business Overview

Trex Company (NYSE: TREX) is the leading U.S. manufacturer of high-performance, low-maintenance composite (wood-alternative) decking and railing products. The brand "Trex" is functionally synonymous with composite decking — a category that has compounded share against pressure-treated wood for 25+ years. FY2025 revenue was ~$1.13B with adjusted EPS of $1.88; the company guided **FY2026 revenue to $1.185B–$1.230B and Adjusted EBITDA of $315M–$340M**, assuming a flat-to-slightly-down repair-and-remodel (R&R) market. Q4 2025 sales were $161M (–4% YoY) but $17M above guide midpoint ([Trex Q4 2025 press release](https://investor.trex.com/news-and-events/news-releases), 2026-02-24).

The company manufactures from recycled polyethylene + reclaimed wood at its Winchester, VA and new Little Rock, AR facilities. Railing has emerged as the strategic growth platform — TREX targets doubling its railing market share by year-end 2028. CEO Bryan Fairbanks plans to retire in late April 2026; COO Adam Zambanini becomes CEO.

## Moat Assessment

**Narrow** — brand + scale + distribution moat in a category that is itself a structural share-taker against pressure-treated wood.

**Sources**:
- **Brand dominance**: "Trex" is the category-defining brand in U.S. composite decking
- **Pro contractor channel**: Established relationships with thousands of decking contractors; the install base creates pull-through
- **Manufacturing scale**: Winchester + Little Rock vertical-integrated polymer + reclaimed-wood pipeline
- **Recycling moat**: ~400M lbs/yr of recycled polyethylene + reclaimed wood — both a cost and ESG advantage

**Vulnerabilities**:

- Big-box distribution (Home Depot, Lowe's) gives major retailers pricing leverage
- Polyethylene + linear-economy raw material cost volatility
- Railing is a less-defended category (more competition, lower margin profile mix)

## Section 1 — Why Does This Company Exist? + Pivotal Investment Question

**Founding insight (Roger Wittenberg, 1996)**: Pressure-treated wood decking rots, splinters, and requires regular maintenance. A composite material made from recycled polyethylene bags + reclaimed wood fiber could deliver a 25–50 year lifespan with minimal maintenance — and turn a waste stream into a high-margin building product. The first product launched in 1996; Home Depot distribution opened the category to consumers.

**Today**: TREX is the U.S. category leader in composite decking, sells via Home Depot + Lowe's + 6,700+ specialty dealers, and has expanded into railing as its second growth platform. The category is ~30% of decking material share, with composite still gaining vs. PT wood every year.

**Pivotal question**: At $42 with a $4.4B market cap and FY26E adj. EBITDA of ~$330M (midpoint), TREX trades at ~13× EV/EBITDA. Is this a deep-value entry into a category compounder where the cycle inflects in 12–18 months, or has the housing-rates regime structurally compressed the long-run growth algorithm?

## Section 9 — Industry-Specific Macro Analysis

**U.S. decking TAM**: ~$8B annually. Composite + PVC at ~30% share with 50bps/yr structural gain. TREX is the leader (~30%+ of composite).

**Housing R&R cycle**: Repair-and-remodel spend was ~$500B in 2024; recent quarters have decelerated as homeowners delay discretionary projects on rates + economic uncertainty. Joint Center for Housing Studies LIRA index implies modest deceleration through mid-2026.

**Mortgage rates**: 30-year mortgage at ~6.5% range; ~50bps reduction would meaningfully unlock housing turnover and decking R&R demand. Rate-cycle inflection is the macro swing factor.

**Aging deck installed base**: Roughly 60M U.S. residential decks; the ~20M aged 15+ years represents replacement-cycle demand floor.
