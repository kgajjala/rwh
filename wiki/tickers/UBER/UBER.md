## Business Overview

Uber Technologies operates a three-segment global on-demand platform: **Mobility** (ride-hailing in 70+ countries), **Delivery** (food, grocery, retail, alcohol, with the Eats brand at the core), and **Freight** (digital broker matching shippers and carriers). The company connects 199M monthly active platform consumers (MAPCs) with millions of drivers/couriers, generating $193.5B in gross bookings in FY2025 ($52B in revenue, $8.7B Adj EBITDA, $9.8B FCF). The business is asset-light: Uber doesn't own vehicles or hire drivers — it operates the matching algorithm, payments rails, trust/safety layer, and consumer-facing app, and takes a fee on every transaction.

The strategic positioning post-2024 has shifted decisively toward **superapp + AV-distribution-layer**. Uber One (subscription) crossed 50M members with member spend 3× non-member, creating a Costco-like flywheel. Simultaneously, Uber has signed 30+ autonomous-vehicle partnerships (Waymo, Zoox, Hertz/Nuro/Lucid, Aurora) — positioning itself as the demand-and-distribution platform that AV operators need to monetize their hardware, rather than competing in AV development directly (Uber sold ATG to Aurora in 2020). The 2026 product expansion into hotels (Expedia partnership, 700K properties) and chauffeur services (Blacklane acquisition) signals a deliberate shift from ride-hailing to vertically-integrated travel platform.

## Pivotal Investment Question

**Does Uber's distribution moat (199M MAPCs, 50M Uber One members, real-time matching across 70+ countries) make it the indispensable layer for autonomous-vehicle commercialization — or does AV economics ultimately let OEMs/Waymo bypass Uber and capture the consumer relationship directly?**

The market priced "bypass" in late 2024 (stock −35% from highs); the 2025–2026 partnership flurry (Waymo Phoenix/Austin, Zoox Vegas, Hertz/Nuro SF) is the rebuttal. Q1 2026's guidance pop is the early scoreboard, not the verdict.

## 3. Competitive Moat & Landscape

### Moat Rating: **Wide (Network Effects + Brand)**

The core moat is the two-sided network — driver supply density determines wait time, wait time determines consumer retention, consumer demand determines driver earnings, which reinforces driver supply. This loop is geographically local (a market with deep supply in NYC tells you nothing about Mumbai), which is why challenger entry is regional and why Uber's 70-country footprint compounds: each new market it enters from a position of platform muscle (capital, operations, brand) is harder to dislodge than a standing-start local entrant.

### Moat Sources

1. **Network density**: Sub-3-minute pickup times in core US/EU markets are an active barrier. A Lyft-only driver in NYC takes a meaningful liquidity discount; the marginal driver chooses Uber for income variance reasons.
2. **Cross-platform leverage (Uber One)**: 50M members spending 3× drives a demand-side switching cost that didn't exist in 2022. Members order ~50% of consolidated bookings — that volume is structurally less price-sensitive.
3. **Data/algorithm advantage**: Pricing, ETA prediction, surge calibration, fraud detection trained on 13.6B trips (FY2025) and ~$1T cumulative GB. New entrants start cold-start.
4. **Capital scale**: $9.8B FY2025 FCF → ability to subsidize new market entry, fund AV partnerships ($XB undisclosed prepayments to AV partners), and absorb regulatory penalties asymmetrically.
5. **Driver multi-homing economics**: Drivers run both Uber and Lyft simultaneously, but Uber's higher utilization means the driver-perceived "share of wallet" tilts to Uber — a soft form of supply-side lock-in.

### Where the Moat Faces Pressure

- **AV disintermediation**: If Waymo, Tesla, or Zoox build direct consumer relationships (own app, own subscription), Uber becomes a mere demand aggregator with no two-sided network advantage. The 30+ AV partnerships are a proactive defense; the Q1 2026 commentary on "Uber Autonomous Solutions" — fleet management, financing, insurance — is explicitly positioning Uber as the operating layer AV operators need.
- **Driver classification**: A reclassification ruling (Prop 22 reversal in CA, EU directive enforcement) raises Mobility cost base 15–20% and breaks the "asset-light platform" framing.
- **Delivery commoditization**: In US Delivery, DoorDash retains >65% share; Uber Eats is the structural #2 with stable but unimpressive economics. The advertising add-on is the bull bet.

The **AV row is the live debate**. The bear case is that AV economics (no driver, no surge labor) generate enough margin to justify the build, and once built, they bypass Uber entirely. Q1 2026's 30+ partnerships including Waymo (the AV leader) is meaningful — but Waymo's own consumer app is also live, and the long-term split is unknowable.

## 7. Industry-Specific Macro Analysis

Uber sits across three macro variable sets — Mobility (consumer discretionary + fuel costs), Delivery (consumer discretionary + restaurant macro), Freight (industrial cycle).

### Dominant Macro Variables (Ranked by Impact on Forward EBITDA)

1. **US/EU consumer discretionary spending** (35–40% sensitivity)
   - Direction Q1 2026: Mixed — labor markets resilient but Q1 saw "macro uncertainty" cited multiple times (gas prices +50% post-Iran, weather)
   - Sensitivity: A 1% drop in real consumer spending = ~1.5% Mobility GB impact; Delivery less elastic (frequency, not basket size)
2. **Gasoline / driver economics** (15–20%)
   - Direction Q1 2026: Adverse — US gas +50% feb-onward post-Iran disruption pressured driver supply
   - Sensitivity: 10% gas price spike = 2–3% driver pay incentive increase needed = 0.5pp Mobility margin pressure (offset by surge pricing)
3. **Insurance costs** (10–15%)
   - Direction 2026: Favorable — "hundreds of millions" of savings in 2026 per management; offloading to third parties
   - Sensitivity: One-time benefit, not recurring; partially passed to consumers as lower fares
4. **FX (international ~40% of revenue)** (5–10%)
   - Direction Q1 2026: Neutral (FY2025 reported = constant currency)
   - Historical headwind in strong-USD years
5. **Fed funds rate / risk-free rate** (valuation, not operations)
   - Direction: Holding steady late 2025–2026
   - Sensitivity: 100bps = ~10–15% multiple impact for long-duration unprofitable-tech peers; UBER is now profitable so less sensitive than peers
6. **Freight rate cycle** (low impact — Freight ~10% of revenue with breakeven margin)
   - Direction Q1 2026: First cyclical inflection in ~2 yrs

### Regulatory Environment (Critical, Non-Macro)

- **Driver classification**: California Prop 22 holds (2024 CA Supreme Court decision favorable); EU Platform Work Directive enforcement begins 2026–2027; UK ruled in favor of worker status 2021 (Uber complied via supplemental benefits without classification change globally)
- **Antitrust**: No active US investigation; EU has historically focused on member-state level interventions
- **AV-specific**: NHTSA regulatory framework still emergent; state-level patchwork (CA, AZ, NV, TX leading); insurance/liability frameworks largely undefined for at-scale deployment
